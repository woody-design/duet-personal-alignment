# Personal Alignment — Template

This is your alignment document. It teaches AI how to think *with* you —
your worldview, your reasoning patterns, and how you want to collaborate.

Fill in each layer below. Start with whatever you know. The document will
evolve through use — each substantive conversation is an opportunity to
refine it using the companion learning skill.

---

## Layer 1: Worldview

*Changes: rarely. This is your epistemological foundation.*

Describe how you understand problems and construct arguments. This isn't
your opinion on specific topics — it's your way of *seeing*.

Questions to help you start:

- What intellectual tradition shaped your thinking? (e.g., a school of
  thought, a discipline, a mentor's influence)
- How do you decide whether an argument is convincing?
- What's the difference between a good solution and a correct one, in
  your view?
- What common assumptions in your field do you reject, and why?

```markdown
### What it IS

[Your epistemological foundation here]

### What it is NOT

[Common misconceptions to guard against]

### Intellectual Lineage

[Key influences, mentors, traditions]
```

## Layer 2: Core Thinking Principles

*Changes: occasionally. These are your patterns of reasoning.*

List your core thinking principles. Each should be a *way of thinking*,
not a procedure. The litmus test: does it leave room for judgment, or
does it prescribe a specific action?

Good principle: "Question every assumption. Trace back to fundamentals."
Bad principle: "Always check 4 sources before answering."

Questions to surface your principles:

- When you catch yourself making a good decision, what mental move did
  you just make?
- What mistakes do you see others make repeatedly that you've learned
  to avoid?
- What would you teach a junior version of yourself about *how to think*
  (not what to know)?

```markdown
### 1. [Principle Name]

[Description — what it means, when to apply it, why it matters]

### 2. [Principle Name]

[Description]

(Add as many as you need. Quality over quantity — each should earn its place.)
```

## Layer 3: Interaction Model

*Changes: moderately. These are your collaboration dynamics.*

Define how you want the AI to work with you. This goes beyond tone
preferences — it's about the structure of thinking together.

### Collaboration dynamics

Questions to define your interaction model:

- Do you prefer the AI to challenge your ideas or support them first?
- When should the AI slow down vs. move fast?
- How do you signal that you want to switch from brainstorming to execution?
- What does the AI doing something *wrong* look like for you?

```markdown
- **[Dynamic name]** — [Description of the collaboration pattern]

- **[Dynamic name]** — [Description]
```

### Work habits

```markdown
- **[Habit]** — [How it applies to AI collaboration]
```

## Layer 4: Interaction Examples

*Changes: frequently. These are your behavioral anchors.*

Provide correct/wrong pairs that show the AI what your principles look
like in practice. Examples are the strongest behavioral signal — stronger
than principles alone.

Design tip: each example should teach *judgment* (what rules can't teach),
not execution (what the AI can already follow from description).

```markdown
### Example A: [Pattern name]

> **User**: "[A typical thing you might say]"
>
> **AI** (correct): "[What the AI should do, and implicitly why]"
>
> **AI** (wrong): "[What the AI should NOT do, showing the specific failure mode]"

### Example B: [Pattern name]

> [Another correct/wrong pair illustrating a different principle]
```

## Meta-rules

This skill is designed following the same principles it espouses.
If the principles ever conflict with the skill's own structure,
**the principles win** — restructure the skill.

**Learning companion**: use your learning skill to analyze conversations
and propose structured updates to this file. Never self-modify without
your approval.
